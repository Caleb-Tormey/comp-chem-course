6 Appendix: A Chemist’s Crash Course in Linear Algebra

If you have ever felt like quantum mechanics is just an excuse for physicists to invent confusing new math, you are not entirely wrong. However, under the hood, almost all of quantum chemistry is driven by a single, incredibly powerful engine: Linear Algebra.

This appendix is a rapid-fire refresher on the mechanics and physical meaning of vectors and matrices. We won’t prove any theorems here; we are just going to look at how to run the machinery and what the outputs actually mean for a chemist. Also, don’t show your math professor this appendix. They will be horrified by the lack of rigor and the hand-waving. But if you are a chemist, this is exactly what you need to survive quantum mechanics and computational chemistry.

6.1 1. Vectors (The States)

In computational chemistry, a vector is simply a list of numbers that describes the state of a system. If you digitize a wavefunction onto a grid of 100 points, that wavefunction is now a 100-dimensional vector.

6.1.1 The Vector Norm (Length)

The norm (or magnitude) of a vector tells you its total length. You calculate it using the multi-dimensional version of the Pythagorean theorem: square all the terms, add them up, and take the square root.

The Math: \[||\vec{v}|| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \dots + v_n^2}\]

The Example: If \(\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix}\): \[||\vec{v}|| = \sqrt{(3)^2 + (-4)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5\]

The Physical Meaning: In quantum mechanics, the total probability of finding a particle somewhere in the universe must be exactly 100% (or 1.0). When we normalize a wavefunction, we are simply dividing the vector by its norm so that its total length equals exactly 1.0.

6.1.2 The Inner Product (The Overlap)

The inner product (or dot product) takes two vectors and crunches them down into a single number. You multiply the corresponding pieces together and add up the results.

The Math: \[\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + \dots + u_n v_n\]

The Example: If \(\vec{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}\): \[\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = (2)(3) + (1)(-2) = 6 - 2 = 4\]

The Physical Meaning: The inner product measures overlap (or similarity). It tells you how much of Vector A points in the same direction as Vector B. * If the inner product is a large positive number, the states overlap significantly. * If the inner product is exactly zero, the vectors are perfectly perpendicular (orthogonal). In quantum mechanics, orthogonal wavefunctions are completely independent states that share absolutely zero overlap (like a \(p_x\) and a \(p_y\) orbital).

6.2 2. Matrices (The Operators)

If vectors are the “nouns” of linear algebra (the states), matrices are the “verbs” (the operators). A matrix does something to a vector—it rotates it, stretches it, or flips it. In quantum mechanics, the Hamiltonian (\(\mathbf{H}\)) is just a giant matrix that operates on our wavefunction.

6.2.1 Matrix-Vector Multiplication

To multiply a matrix by a vector, you take the dot product of each row of the matrix with the column of the vector.

The Math: \[\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} ax + by \\ cx + dy \end{bmatrix}\]

The Example: Let’s see what happens when an operator acts on a state vector: \[\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (2)(4) + (1)(2) \\ (0)(4) + (3)(2) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 8 + 2 \\ 0 + 6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}\]

The Physical Meaning: The matrix grabbed the original state \(\begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\) and transformed it into an entirely new state \(\begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}\). The only time the matrix doesn’t change the fundamental direction of the vector is if that vector happens to be an eigenvector!

6.2.2 The Determinant

The determinant is a single number that summarizes the overall “scaling power” of a square matrix.

The Math (\(2 \times 2\) example): To find the determinant of a \(2 \times 2\) matrix, you cross-multiply the diagonals and subtract them. \[\text{det}\left(\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\right) = ad - bc\]

The Example: \[\text{det}\left(\begin{bmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\right) = (5)(3) - (2)(1) = 15 - 2 = 13\]

The Physical Meaning: Think of a matrix as stretching or shrinking the fabric of space. The determinant tells you the exact factor by which areas (or volumes) are scaled during that transformation. * A determinant of \(13\) means the matrix stretches 2D space to be 13 times larger. * A determinant of \(1\) means the volume stays exactly the same (like a simple rotation). * If the determinant is exactly zero, it means the matrix is a trash compactor. It crushes 2D space down into a flat 1D line (or 3D space into a 2D plane). All volume is destroyed, information is lost, and the transformation cannot be reversed (the matrix is non-invertible).

## 2. Matrices (The Operators)

**The Math:**
$$\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} ax + by \\ cx + dy \end{bmatrix}$$

**The Example:**
Let's see what happens when an operator acts on a state vector:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (2)(4) + (1)(2) \\ (0)(4) + (3)(2) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 8 + 2 \\ 0 + 6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}$$

**The Example:**
$$\text{det}\left(\begin{bmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\right) = (5)(3) - (2)(1) = 15 - 2 = 13$$
