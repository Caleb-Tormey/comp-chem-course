8.1 The Physical System: The Double-Well Potential

The Nitrogen atom in ammonia experiences a symmetric double-well potential as it flips back and forth through the plane of Hydrogen atoms. We can model this coordinate \(x\) using a Quartic Double-Well Potential:

\[V(x) = A (x^2 - x_0^2)^2\]

  • \(x = \pm x_0\) represent the two stable equilibrium geometries (Nitrogen “up” vs. Nitrogen “down”).
  • \(x = 0\) is the planar transition state with a barrier height of \(V_{\text{max}} = A x_0^4\).

8.1.1 Physical Parameters (Atomic Units: \(\hbar = m = 1\))

Use the following parameters for your simulation: * Grid span: \(x \in [-4.0, 4.0]\) with \(N = 400\) grid points. * Well minima location: \(x_0 = 1.5\) * Potential prefactor: \(A = 1.0\) (This yields a barrier height \(V_{\text{max}} \approx 5.06\) at \(x=0\)).

8.3 Task 2: Calculating Tunneling Splitting and Inversion Frequency

Because the central barrier is finite, the ground state level splits into a pair of nearly degenerate states: * \(E_1\): Symmetric ground state (even parity) * \(E_2\): Antisymmetric first excited state (odd parity)

The energy gap \(\Delta E = E_2 - E_1\) dictates the quantum tunneling frequency: \[\nu = \frac{\Delta E}{h} = \frac{\Delta E}{2\pi \hbar}\]

In atomic units (\(\hbar = 1\)), the tunneling frequency is simply \(\nu = \frac{\Delta E}{2\pi}\).

8.4 Task 3: The Classical vs. Quantum Comparison

Classically, a particle trapped in one of the wells cannot cross to the other side unless it receives enough thermal energy to jump over the barrier.

According to Arrhenius/Transition State Theory, the thermal rate constant \(k_{\text{thermal}}\) to jump over a barrier of height \(V_{\text{max}}\) at temperature \(T\) is proportional to the Boltzmann factor: \[k_{\text{thermal}} \approx \nu_0 e^{-V_{\text{max}} / (k_B T)}\]

Where \(\nu_0\) is the vibrational frequency inside a single well (\(\approx 0.5\) a.u.), \(k_B = 3.166 \times 10^{-6} \text{ Hartree/K}\), and \(T = 300 \text{ K}\).
